Fundamental Rights

Article 14 – Right to Equality

Article 14 guarantees equality before law and equal protection of laws. It ensures that every citizen is treated equally regardless of religion, caste, gender or status. The principle of rule of law prevents arbitrary government actions. Reasonable classification is allowed but discrimination is prohibited. The judiciary ensures enforcement through judicial review. Equality strengthens democracy and ensures fairness in governance.

Article 19 – Right to Freedom

Article 19 grants six essential freedoms including speech, expression, assembly, movement, residence and profession. These freedoms enable participation in democracy. Reasonable restrictions exist to protect sovereignty, public order and morality. Freedom of speech allows constructive criticism of government policies. Freedom of profession ensures economic independence.

Article 21 – Right to Life and Liberty

Article 21 ensures protection of life and personal liberty. It includes right to dignity, privacy, clean environment and legal aid. No person can be deprived of liberty without due process of law. It is one of the most powerful protections in the Constitution.

Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)

Fundamental Duties were added by the 42nd Constitutional Amendment in 1976. These duties remind citizens that rights come with responsibilities. Every citizen must respect the Constitution, National Flag and National Anthem. Citizens must protect sovereignty, unity and integrity of India. It is the duty of every individual to promote harmony and brotherhood beyond religious, linguistic or regional differences. Protecting environment including forests, lakes and wildlife is also a constitutional duty. Citizens must safeguard public property and avoid violence. Developing scientific temper, humanism and spirit of inquiry is essential for national progress. Parents or guardians must provide opportunities for education to children between 6 and 14 years of age. Fundamental Duties are not directly enforceable by courts but they guide responsible citizenship. A strong democracy depends not only on rights but also on disciplined and aware citizens.

Anti-Corruption Laws of India

Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988

The Prevention of Corruption Act criminalizes bribery and misuse of official position. Public servants accepting illegal gratification can be prosecuted. The Act was amended in 2018 to strengthen provisions and make bribery of public servants punishable. It aims to maintain integrity in public administration.

Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013

This Act established the Lokpal at the central level and Lokayuktas in states to investigate corruption complaints against public officials including ministers. It promotes transparency and accountability in governance.

Right to Information Act, 2005

The RTI Act empowers citizens to request information from public authorities. It enhances transparency and reduces corruption by making government accountable to the public.
